How do we know the cosy score is actually good?

The honest way to test a scorer — explained simply, then properly. (a.k.a. “k-fold”)

Part 1 — Explain it like I’m 5

🧸 the flashcard story

You have 100 spelling flashcards. You practice with all 100. Then your “test” is… those same 100 cards. You get every one right! 🎉

But wait — did you actually learn to spell, or did you just memorise those exact 100 words? Nobody knows. The test was too easy because you’d already seen every answer.

To find out if you really learned, someone has to test you on words you’ve never practiced.

k-fold is a fair way to do that: split the flashcards into a few piles. Practice with most of the piles, then test yourself on the one pile you didn’t practice with. Then swap which pile is the test, and do it again, until every card has had a turn being a “surprise” test card. Your real score is how you did on the surprise cards.

Part 2 — Why this matters for our cosy score

Our scorer has a cheat sheet — on purpose

When the cosy scorer rates a hotel, it’s allowed to peek at your human grades for similar hotels and copy your taste. That’s the calibration — and it’s a good thing. It’s how the score learns your idea of cosy.

The problem is only when we measure how good the scorer is. We check its scores against your human grades. But if the hotel it just scored had its own grade sitting on the cheat sheet, then it basically saw the answer before answering. Of course it matches!

That’s what “in-sample” means, and it’s why our current “miss of 1.35 points” is a little too flattering — part of it is the scorer reciting answers it was shown, not predicting fresh ones.

“Held-out” = hide the answer, then test

A held-out test means: take some grades, hide them from the cheat sheet, score those hotels, and only then compare to the hidden grades. Now the scorer can’t cheat, so the score you get is honest. k-fold is just a clever way to do this using all your grades, not wasting any.

After 5 rounds, every hotel has had exactly one turn being scored honestly (with its answer hidden). Average all those misses together → that’s the honest MAE. No grade was wasted, and nothing cheated.

“k” is just how many folds. 5-fold = 5 piles. 10-fold = 10 piles. The extreme version, “leave-one-out,” hides just one hotel at a time (112 rounds) — the most thorough, the most work.

One catch for us: twins (“grouped” k-fold)

Our catalog has lots of near-duplicate hotels (same chain, or the same place listed twice). Imagine the “Ibis Budapest” is in the test fold, but its twin “Ibis Budapest (2)” is in the cheat sheet fold. The scorer peeks at the twin’s grade… and basically sees the test answer anyway. 😬

The fix: put twins in the same fold, so a hotel can never peek at its own near-copy. That’s called grouped k-fold. We need it because ~27% of the catalog were duplicates.

Part 4 — There’s a second way (your swipe idea)

🔵 a fresh test set

Instead of cleverly reusing the old grades, you could just gather brand-new human judgments on hotels that were never on the cheat sheet — for example, a fresh round of friend swipes on the hotels we actually show on city pages.

Because those hotels were never used to calibrate, they’re a held-out test by definition — no folds, no hiding needed. And it tests the hotels that matter (the ones travellers see), with several friends averaging out any one person’s quirks.

k-fold = squeeze an honest number out of the grades we already have, for free, today.
Fresh swipes = the cleaner, more relevant number, but it needs people to swipe. They’re complementary — not either/or.

Part 5 — Quick check (no pressure 🙂)

Question 1

Why does testing yourself on the same 100 flashcards you studied give a misleadingly high score?

Show answer
Because you might have just memorised those exact words rather than learning to spell. A high score on cards you’ve already seen doesn’t prove you can handle new ones — which is the whole point.
Question 2

In 5-fold, how many times does each hotel get to be a “surprise test” hotel?

Show answer
Exactly once. That’s the trick — across the 5 rounds, every hotel is hidden-and-tested one time, and used as cheat-sheet the other four times. So every grade contributes, and every hotel gets one honest test.
Question 3

Why do we put duplicate/twin hotels in the same fold?

Show answer
So a hotel can’t secretly peek at its own twin’s grade on the cheat sheet. If twins are split across folds, the “honest” test isn’t honest anymore — the answer leaked through the near-copy.
Question 4

Your friends swipe on 30 brand-new cosy hotels we’ve never graded and never put on the cheat sheet. Is that in-sample or held-out?

Show answer
Held-out — and it’s the cleanest kind. The scorer never saw those answers during calibration, so comparing its scores to the fresh swipes is a totally fair test. (This is exactly your swipe idea.)
Question 5 — the real decision

We want to know how trustworthy the score is on the hotels travellers actually see. Which gives a more relevant answer: k-fold on the old grades, or a fresh swipe round on the surfaced hotels?

Show answer
The fresh swipe round — because the old grades were mostly collected on borderline / bad hotels (to catch over-scores), not the cosy ones we’re proud to show. A fresh round on the surfaced hotels measures the thing you actually care about. k-fold is still useful as a free, instant number from existing data while the swipes come in.
